The engines, in plain terms

Loose — the trigger-happy baseline. Nearly every 5-minute round, it buys whichever side BTC is leaning toward, as long as the basics check out (7 of 10 guards + a steady quote) and the price is 65¢ or less. Takes almost everything, good and bad.

Floor — Loose plus one rule: the move has to be real. It only buys if BTC has already moved at least 0.02% off the round's open. Skips the dead-flat rounds — those are coin flips, and coin flips minus fees lose.

Band — Floor plus one more rule: …but not too big (0.04% max). If BTC has made a big move and you can still buy that side for 65¢ or less, it's cheap because the market doubts the move — and the market is usually right. Band only buys the goldilocks moves: big enough to be real, small enough that the cheap price is honest.

Value — the fair-value engine. Instead of the flat 65¢ cap, it estimates the side's own chance of holding to the close (from the move so far, seconds left, and recent volatility) and only buys when the price is below that by 3¢. It goes after the underpriced sides the book is slow to mark. Added later like Fade, so its line starts partway across the chart and its sample is small.

Fade — the contrarian. It does the exact opposite of Loose: whenever Loose buys a side, Fade buys the other one at its own book price. The bet is that Loose overpays for the favorite, so taking the underdog against it should win a little more than it costs. Added later than the other four, so its line starts partway across the chart and its sample is still small.

Strict — the original by-the-book strategy: needs all 10 guards green at the same moment, which almost never happens. Barely trades; kept as a reference point.

Reversal — the odd one out, and the most interesting. Every other engine bets on the move continuing; this one bets it reverses. When the interval that just ended made a big move (≥0.12%), Reversal buys the opposite side of the new interval right at the open — while it's still cheap near 50¢. The idea: big 5-minute moves overshoot and snap back. In 10 days of history that happened ~56% of the time, which at a 50¢ price is a real edge. It's brand new, paper-only, and being watched live to see if the history holds. Not enabled for real trading.

Reversal2 — the same bet as Reversal, but a looser trigger finger. Reversal sometimes sits out because the order book is empty on one side in the first seconds after the open, and it won't guess a price. Reversal2 fills that gap: when the book is thin it uses the market's mid-price (~50¢) and jumps in anyway. Runs side by side with Reversal so we can measure how many good setups the stricter one lets slip — and whether jumping in on a thin book helps or hurts. Also paper-only.

Latent Fire — Reversal with a brain about when to fire. Reversal only works when big moves snap back, which happens in choppy markets and fails badly in trending ones. So this one watches the last hour and measures how directional the market has been (a trend-efficiency score). When the market is grinding one direction, it stays latent and does nothing. When the market is chopping back and forth, it fires the reversal bet. In testing, this gate turned a losing always-on reversal book into a winning one by simply sitting out the trends. It is the newest idea and the closest thing to a regime-aware hybrid. Paper-only.

Loose, Floor, and Band each add exactly one rule to the one before — so the P&L gap between those three is the dollar value of that single rule. Value (a fair-value gate), Fade (a contrarian), Reversal, Reversal2 & Latent Fire (a cross-interval mean-reversion bet, its jump-in-anyway twin, and its regime-gated version), and Strict (a stricter reference) sit outside that chain. All see the exact same markets.
